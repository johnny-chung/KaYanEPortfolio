    Section 1

    Diversity

    As defined by the College of ECE (2017), diversity refers to “Differences and uniqueness that each person brings to the early learning and care setting including values and beliefs, culture and ethnicity, language, ability, education, life experiences, socioeconomic status, spirituality, gender, age, and sexual orientation (p. 23). As an ECE student and future educator, it is important to learn, understand and respect the uniqueness and difference of children and families in relation to different social backgrounds, cultures values, belief and experience. When planning and implementing learning activities and understanding and responding to ideas, choices in play, and experiences, it is essential to recognize and include cultures, backgrounds and experiences of children and families (College of ECE, 2020).

    In my field placement, with respect and understanding of the uniqueness of children from different cultural and social backgrounds, I help children develop a sense of belonging and learn to appreciate different cultures by creating an inclusive and responsive environment with various learning activities infused with values of diversity and cultures. For example, in circle time, I have read some story books such as “Skin Like Mine” (Perry & Jackson, 2016) and “Where Are You From?” (Méndez & Kim, 2019) with elements of multi-cultures and have interactive discussions with children to see how the lives of characters are different from and similar to their daily lives.     Anti-racism

    Anti-racism refers to the ongoing process of recognizing and addressing racism by challenging and changing current systems, structures, policies, practices, behaviours, attitudes and values, so that power, wealth and resources can be reallocated and shared equitably (CARED, n.d.). To challenge and eliminate racism that is deep-rooted in society leading to discrimination and inequality suffered by the racial groups including people of colour and Indigenous people, one of the effective strategies is to start to make changes in early childhood education learning environment. As children become aware of racial and cultural differences and may be easily affected by biases learnt from the environment when they are small, there is a need to help children acquire “experiences that develop anti-bias attitudes, engage them in critical pedagogy, and teach them how to speak out against injustice” (Iruka et al., 2020, p. 75). It is important for educators to start from reflecting own biases and practices, examine curriculum materials to identify and address racial bias, and developing anti-bias and anti-racism education.

    Therefore, in my field placement, I have brought and read the book of “All the Colors We Are” regarding physical difference about different race and the reasons behind (Kissinger, 2014) and the storybook of “The Proudest Blue: A Story of Hijab and Family?” about cultural and racial difference (Muhammad et. al., 2019) with children. We have conducted active and interactive sharing by discussing questions like “why people have different skin colour”, “how are we different from each other”, “what does Hijab mean?”, “why the children laugh at the girl wearing Hijab” and “What can we do if your were the girl?”. In this way, it helps children learn to understand the differences and similarities of self and others with empathy, interact with others in a fair and inclusive attitude and respond to biases with positive emotions. By creating an inclusive and caring environment with implementation of anti-bias and anti-racism education starting from early stage, children will gradually learn to respond to biases and challenge discrimination and inequity that the communities of color and minorities encounter in unbiased and positive ways.
